Summary of Matthew

The Gospel of Matthew presents Jesus Christ as Israel's promised Messiah and King. Written primarily for a Jewish audience, it emphasizes how Jesus fulfills Old Testament prophecies. Key themes include the Kingdom of Heaven (or Kingdom of God), Jesus' authority as the Son of God, righteousness, discipleship, and the inclusion of Gentiles into God's plan.

Key Themes:

  • Jesus as the Messiah and King
  • The Kingdom of Heaven
  • Fulfillment of Old Testament Prophecy
  • Righteousness and the Law
  • Discipleship and the Cost
  • The Authority of Jesus

Key Events & Sections:

  • Genealogy and Birth of Jesus (Ch 1-2)
  • Baptism and Temptation of Jesus (Ch 3-4)
  • Sermon on the Mount (Ch 5-7)
  • Miracles demonstrating Jesus' authority (Ch 8-9)
  • Commissioning of the Twelve Disciples (Ch 10)
  • Parables of the Kingdom (Ch 13)
  • Peter's Confession and the Transfiguration (Ch 16-17)
  • Teaching on Divorce, Wealth, and Service (Ch 19-20)
  • Triumphal Entry and Temple Cleansing (Ch 21)
  • Olivet Discourse (End Times Prophecy) (Ch 24-25)
  • The Last Supper, Betrayal, and Arrest (Ch 26)
  • Trial, Crucifixion, and Death of Jesus (Ch 27)
  • Resurrection and the Great Commission (Ch 28)

Key Characters:

  • Jesus Christ
  • The Twelve Apostles (Simon Peter, Andrew, James, John, Philip, Bartholomew, Thomas, Matthew, James son of Alphaeus, Thaddaeus, Simon the Zealot, Judas Iscariot)
  • John the Baptist
  • Mary and Joseph
  • Pharisees and Sadducees
  • Pontius Pilate
  • Herod the Great / Herod Antipas

Key Teachings:

  • The Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3-12)
  • The Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13)
  • The Golden Rule (Matthew 7:12)
  • Parables explaining the Kingdom (e.g., Sower, Mustard Seed, Hidden Treasure)
  • Instructions on prayer, fasting, giving, forgiveness, and judging others.
  • The Great Commission: Making disciples of all nations (Matthew 28:19-20)

Historical Context

Authorship:

Traditionally attributed to the Apostle Matthew, also known as Levi, a former tax collector called by Jesus to be one of the twelve disciples (Matthew 9:9).

Date Written:

Scholarly estimates vary, but a likely range is between 60 AD and 85 AD.

Audience:

Primarily written for a Jewish Christian audience. This is evident from the frequent references to Old Testament prophecies, Jewish customs, and terminology (like "Kingdom of Heaven" instead of "Kingdom of God," possibly out of reverence for God's name).

Purpose:

To demonstrate that Jesus of Nazareth is the promised Messiah, the King of the Jews, who fulfilled the prophecies of the Old Testament. It serves as a manual for discipleship, outlining the ethical teachings and mission given by Jesus.

Cultural Significance:

Matthew's Gospel bridges the Old and New Testaments, showing continuity in God's redemptive plan. It has profoundly shaped Christian understanding of Jesus' identity, teachings (especially ethics via the Sermon on the Mount), and the mission of the Church (the Great Commission).
